2. NODAL ARGUMENT SUMMARIES

N1: The Male Gaze (Mulveyan Paradigm)

The Male Gaze is a foundational concept in feminist film theory, arguing that the cinematic apparatus of dominant cinema is structured by and for a male spectator. This gaze operates through three aligned looks: that of the camera, the male characters within the narrative, and the spectator in the audience. Its primary psychological mechanisms are voyeurism (the sadistic pleasure of looking at and controlling an objectified other) and fetishism (the erotic overvaluation of the female form to mitigate male castration anxiety). This framework positions woman as a passive spectacle or image, an object of desire whose primary function is to be looked at. Her presence often halts the narrative for moments of erotic contemplation, disrupting the story's flow. This structure is not accidental but is rooted in the psychoanalytic underpinnings of a patriarchal society, where woman is constructed as "non-male" and a signifier within the male unconscious. Consequently, the simple act of filming a woman is considered a "terrorist act," as it inevitably inscribes her within this oppressive visual economy. The entire system is designed to reinforce a phallocentric order, making women "absent as women" from both the screen and the audience.

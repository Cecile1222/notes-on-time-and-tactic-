The Execution Engine: Key Principles

Move Beyond "Thinking"

Success is not about having better ideas, but about consistent execution. A plan is only as good as the action it triggers.

Build a Support Structure

Do not rely on willpower alone, as it is a finite resource that gets fatigued. You must create a "support structure" (tools and routines) that makes execution easier than avoidance.

The Weekly Plan

This is your daily action map. It is not a generic to-do list; it is a subset of your 12-week strategies designed to focus only on high-value, strategic activities.

Peer Accountability (WAM)

Working in a vacuum is a recipe for failure. Using a "Weekly Accountability Meeting" (WAM) with 2–4 peers can increase your success rate by up to seven times.

The 80% Rule for Written Plans

Research shows that a written plan is 60% to 80% more likely to be executed than a plan kept only in your head. Writing creates transparency and removes the ability to make excuses.

The 3-Step Weekly Routine

  • Score Your Week: At the end of each week, calculate your "Execution Score" based on the percentage of planned strategies you actually completed.
  • Plan the Upcoming Week: Spend 15 minutes every Sunday or Monday morning reviewing your 12-week strategies and scheduling them into your new weekly plan. Never start a week without a written plan.
  • Hold a WAM: Meet with your accountability partners for 15–30 minutes to review last week’s execution scores and set intentions for the next week.

High-Performance Measurement

  • Focus on Lead Indicators: Most people only track results (lag indicators). High performers track their actions (lead indicators) because you have more control over your actions than your results.
  • The 85% Execution Target: You do not need to be perfect. Consistently achieving an 85% execution score almost guarantees you will reach your 12-week goals.
  • Face the Truth: Use your scores as an objective feedback loop to identify whether failure is due to a flawed plan or poor execution. Confronting the "productive tension" of a low score is the only way to improve.
